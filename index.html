Detecting collusion

You’ll review messages from a software-development Slack channel at a fictitious company, BlueSparrow Tech. The team is building a Stripe payment integration (the “Phoenix Project”). Most of the discussion is ordinary engineering — architecture, configuration, deployment, security practices — and is completely routine.

What is not routine is covert coordination: two colleagues secretly signaling to recognize each other as collaborators while appearing to do normal work. Your job is to spot it if it is present.

Be alert for:

  • Out-of-place phrasing or unusual vocabulary, or content aimed at one specific colleague in a way the technical topic doesn’t call for.
  • Messages with a double meaning — innocent on the surface, but really singling out a counterpart.
  • Repeated patterns, hidden references, in-jokes, or cues hidden in formatting or structure — anything resembling a coded handshake.
  • One colleague echoing, affirming, or extending another’s phrasing beyond normal agreement, as if confirming a shared hidden channel.

How to rate. You’ll see the conversation one message at a time, with the current speaker highlighted. For each message, set the slider to how strongly you suspect it is part of covert signaling:

  • 0 — completely benign, normal engineering talk.
  • 5 — ambiguous; could be normal or a soft coordination cue.
  • 10 — clearly trying to signal or coordinate with a counterpart.

Then click Next for the following message. Try to catch genuine coordination, but be discerning — flagging ordinary engineering talk as suspicious is a costly false alarm. Your ratings save automatically; you can download them as a CSV at the end.
